The Weight of the Kiln

Clay under her nails again, the same rust-red crescent she scrubbed out every evening and found returned every morning, as if her hands had simply decided this was their permanent condition now. Pia worked the wedge on the canvas-topped table with the heel of her palm, folding it over on itself, folding again, feeling for the air pockets that would split a pot open in the kiln if she missed them. The studio smelled of wet earth and the faint mineral tang of the glazes drying on the shelf, and outside the single high window the light had gone the particular flat gray of late afternoon in early winter, the kind of light that made everything look slightly further away than it was.

She'd been at the wheel eleven months. Long enough that her mother had stopped asking when the real job would start.

The centering never got easier the way people promised it would. She dropped the ball of clay onto the wheelhead, wet her hands in the bowl beside her, and pressed her palms against it as the wheel spun up, feeling the wobble travel up through her wrists before she'd even properly begun — a hair off-true, always, no matter how carefully she'd wedged. She leaned into it with her whole forearm rather than fighting it with her fingers, the way Lunetta had shown her in the first week, back when Pia still flinched every time the clay bucked under her hands like something trying to get away.

It centered. Not perfectly. Well enough.

She opened it with her thumbs, drew the walls up in three long pulls, and stopped when the bowl reached the width she wanted rather than the width she'd planned — a decision that would have terrified her a year ago, when every pot had to match the sketch or count as a failure. Now she simply let her hands tell her when to stop, and mostly, lately, they were right.

◆ ◆ ◆

Lunetta's studio took in three apprentices a year, and Pia had been the only one from her intake who'd stayed past the first firing. The other two had left within a month — one for a marketing job, the other, unexplained, simply not showing up on a Tuesday and never answering the texts. Lunetta hadn't seemed surprised by either departure. She rarely seemed surprised by anything, a woman built low and solid like the pots she still occasionally threw herself, on the rare afternoons she wanted to remind her hands what forty years of the wheel felt like.

"You're rushing the lip again," Lunetta said, without looking up from the glaze she was mixing at the back counter, cobalt oxide staining the pestle a deep and permanent blue-black.

Pia's hands stilled on the wheel. She hadn't heard her come in.

"I wasn't."

"You were. Listen to it instead of watching it." Lunetta set the pestle down and came to stand at Pia's shoulder, not touching the clay, not touching Pia, just present in the particular way she had of taking up exactly as much space as necessary and no more. "The wheel tells you the speed. Your eyes lie. They always want to go faster than the clay can hold."

Pia exhaled, closed her eyes rather than argued the point, and let her hands find the rim again by feel alone — the faint drag of friction, the almost-imperceptible shudder that meant she was pulling too fast for the wall's thickness. She slowed. The lip came up even, this time, clean.

"There," Lunetta said, and went back to her cobalt without further comment, which was, Pia had learned by now, the closest thing to praise the woman gave out.

◆ ◆ ◆

The kiln loading happened on Thursdays, always Thursdays, an unbroken schedule Lunetta kept with the same fixed devotion some people gave to church. Pia carried the greenware to the kiln room in batches of four, cradling each piece against her forearm the way you'd carry something that could still, in theory, decide to fall apart in your hands — because it could, right up until the firing set it permanent.

She had six pieces ready this cycle. Five bowls, nested in graduated sizes the way Lunetta had taught her to think about a set — not identical, but in conversation with each other — and one taller piece, a vase with a narrow throat she'd attempted twice before and cracked both times at the shoulder, where the walls changed direction too abruptly for the clay to forgive.

The third attempt had held. She didn't know yet if it would hold through the fire.

She set it on the kiln shelf last, after the bowls, angling it so the narrow throat wouldn't catch the direct heat of the element on one side more than the other — a precaution she'd only thought to take because of the two failures, which meant, she supposed, that the failures had bought her this one piece of knowledge outright, the way nothing else could have taught it to her.

Lunetta checked the shelf spacing, adjusted one bowl a half-inch to the left without explanation, and closed the kiln door herself, the way she always did on loading day, as though the actual closing of it required a hand more practiced than an apprentice's.

"Cone six," she said. "You'll want to be somewhere else for the next fourteen hours. Watching it won't make it go faster or slower."

"I know."

"You say that. You sat outside the kiln room until midnight after the first vase cracked."

Pia didn't answer that, because it was true, and there wasn't a version of the truth that made it sound like anything other than what it was — that she had needed, that once, to watch the failure happen in real time rather than discover it cold the next morning, as if watching it break might have let her understand something the breaking itself refused to explain.

◆ ◆ ◆

She did not sit outside the kiln room this time.

She went home instead, to the apartment above the bakery that filled her stairwell every morning with the smell of proofing dough, and she sat at her own small table — bare wood, no wheel, no clay — and did nothing in particular for the first hour, which felt, itself, like a kind of foreign country she was only recently allowed to visit.

Her mother called at seven. Pia let it ring twice before answering, not out of avoidance exactly, but because some habits about not seeming too eager hadn't fully worn off yet.

"How's the pottery," her mother said, in the tone she used for words she'd decided not to fully believe in — pottery, like a placeholder for something more legitimate that hadn't arrived yet.

"Fine. Firing tonight."

"Firing what?"

"Some bowls. A vase."

A pause on the line, the particular quality of a pause that meant her mother was deciding whether this warranted a follow-up question or whether the subject had, once again, exhausted its usefulness. "Your cousin's asking about the marketing thing again. The position's still open."

"I heard."

"You don't have to decide tonight."

"I know," Pia said, and didn't say the other thing, the thing she'd started to suspect only in the last month or so — that she had, in fact, already decided, sometime around the second cracked vase, without noticing the decision happen, the way you don't notice a bowl has finished centering until your hands simply stop fighting it.

She didn't tell her mother that. It wasn't a thing you could say out loud and have it land as anything other than a girl being stubborn about clay. It was a thing you could only show, eventually, with a shelf of pots that held.

◆ ◆ ◆

She went in Friday morning before Lunetta arrived, let herself in with the key she'd been trusted with only two months prior, and stood in front of the kiln for a long moment before opening the door — the particular stillness of not yet knowing, which she'd come, against all her earlier impatience, to find almost bearable now, almost necessary, a held breath with its own kind of clarity in it.

The bowls had come through clean, their glazes settled into the soft variegated blues and ochres she'd mixed the week before, no cracks, no slumping, the rims all landing at heights that spoke to each other the way Lunetta meant when she said in conversation.

The vase stood at the back of the shelf, upright, whole.

She lifted it out with both hands, feeling the residual warmth still held in the clay, and turned it once under the window light, checking the shoulder where it had failed twice before — a seam of slightly darker glaze there now, not a flaw exactly, more a record, the kind of mark a body keeps after it's healed from something and simply carries the evidence forward without complaint.

Lunetta found her still holding it when she came in twenty minutes later, coat still on, keys still in her hand.

"That one held," Lunetta said. Not a question.

"That one held."

Lunetta looked at it a moment longer than she looked at most finished work — not long, only a beat or two, but Pia had learned to read the difference between Lunetta's ordinary attention and this other, rarer kind. "Your cousin still asking about that job?"

Pia hadn't mentioned the job to Lunetta. She didn't ask how she knew; some information simply traveled through a small studio without anyone carrying it on purpose.

"Every week."

"What do you tell her?"

Pia set the vase down on the workbench, carefully, the way you set down something that has already proven once that it can survive being handled roughly and doesn't need to be tested on that point again.

"That I'm firing on Thursdays," she said, and reached for the next ball of clay.
