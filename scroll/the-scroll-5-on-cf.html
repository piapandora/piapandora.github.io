The Lighthouse Watch

Rust had eaten through the railing in three places, and Pia knew each one by feel now, the way she knew the give of the fourth stair before it groaned. She gripped the sound section and swung around it rather than trust the flaking one beside it, boots skidding on wet iron. Below her, forty feet down, the sea worked at the rocks with the patient, grinding hunger of something that had been doing this since long before the lighthouse existed and would keep doing it long after.

The lamp room smelled of paraffin and cold brass. She'd have to trim the wick again before dusk — the light had guttered twice last night, and both times something out past the reef had answered the dark with a sound like a held breath being let go all at once, a sound that carried wrong across open water, too low, too long.

Her father's logbook sat open on the shelf where he'd left it, the last entry dated eleven days back in handwriting that grew more cramped toward the bottom of the page, as though the hand writing it had been trying to finish before something reached the door.

Light failed 9 nights running. Something in the water reads the dark better than it should. Do not go down to the landing after —

The sentence stopped there. No period. Pia had read it perhaps forty times since, always stopping at the same broken edge, always feeling the same drop in her stomach, like a stair that wasn't where her foot expected it.

She had come out on the supply boat with two months of oil and a rifle she didn't fully trust, and the boat had left without her the moment the crates were unloaded, the ferryman crossing himself in a way he didn't bother to hide.

The wick caught on the third strike of the match, and Pia watched the flame climb steady inside the glass before she closed the lamp housing and set the mechanism turning. The gears took the weight with a groan that ran down through the tower's iron bones, and far below, the beam swept out across black water for the first time that evening, catching whitecaps, catching nothing, catching whitecaps again.

She counted the rotations the way her father had taught her, tapping two fingers against her thigh, because a lighthouse keeper who lost count of the sweep had already lost the only thing standing between the light and the dark it was built to answer.

On the eleventh rotation the beam caught something that was not a whitecap.

It surfaced perhaps sixty yards off the landing, low and long, longer than any boat hull she'd seen in these waters, its back breaking the swell in a single unbroken line before the light swept past and lost it. Pia's hand found the rifle stock without her telling it to. Her palm was slick. She wiped it dry against her coat, once, deliberately, before she let herself grip the wood again.

She did not go down to the landing.

The sound came again near midnight — that same held-breath exhale, rolling in off the water and up through the stone of the tower until she felt it as much as heard it, a pressure behind her sternum. She kept her eyes on the lamp's flame, feeding it in small measured pours from the oil can, because a hand occupied with a task was a hand that couldn't shake, and a keeper who could not stop her hands shaking was no keeper at all.

By the time gray light came up over the water, she had trimmed the wick four times and refilled the reservoir twice, and the log entry she wrote — Light held. Sighting confirmed, approx. 60 yards, landing side — came out in handwriting steadier than she felt.

◆ ◆ ◆

She found the first proof of what she was dealing with two days later, at low tide, when the reef stood half-exposed and slick with weed the color of a bruise. A fishing dinghy lay overturned against the rocks below the landing, its hull stove in from beneath rather than struck from the side, the planks bent inward in a pattern that made her think, unwillingly, of a ribcage crushed by something that had been underneath it the whole time it thought it was simply floating.

There were no bodies. There rarely were, her father's log suggested, in the sparse entries from years back that mentioned this water at all — entries she'd skimmed past a hundred times without weight until now, entries about boats gone missing near the reef in years divisible cleanly by nothing she could find a pattern in, except that they always went missing when the light had failed first.

The light had failed for her father nine nights running.

Pia stood over the broken dinghy a long while, working a splinter of wet wood between her fingers, turning it the way she might turn a fact she didn't want but couldn't put down. The thing did not attack ships. It attacked ships whose lights had already gone dark. It was not hunting the water. It was hunting the failure in the light — or perhaps, she thought, turning the splinter once more before letting it drop, it was only ever hunting whatever the dark handed to it, and the light was simply the one thing standing between her and becoming something handed over.

She climbed back up the iron stair to the lamp room and checked the reservoir levels with a thoroughness she hadn't managed since her first week alone. Two months of oil. She counted the days remaining against the days the boat wouldn't return, and the arithmetic came out uglier than she wanted.

◆ ◆ ◆

The gale hit on the ninth night, exactly as many nights as her father's log recorded before his hand had stopped writing.

Wind took the tower like a fist closing slowly, and rain came sideways hard enough to hiss against the lamp room glass, and somewhere in the black churn beyond the beam's reach the sea was doing something enormous to itself, heaving in swells that Pia felt as a slow, sickening lean in the floor beneath her boots. The gear mechanism that turned the lamp began to catch, a stutter every third rotation, iron teeth grinding against grit that had worked its way in from the storm.

She got the housing open with fingers gone clumsy from cold, and found the fault at once — sand and salt crust jamming the smaller gear, the same fault her father's log had described in his last legible weeks, the fault he had apparently never solved.

Pia did not have a brush fine enough for the gear teeth. She had her own fingernails, and the rifle's cleaning rod, and forty minutes of failing light before the storm would finish what the grit had started.

She worked the rod between the teeth by lamp-glow and feel alone, blood rising thin and dark from a torn nail she didn't stop to acknowledge, because stopping cost more than the pain did. Outside, past the shriek of the wind, she heard it again — the long low exhale, closer now, close enough that it seemed to come from directly beneath the tower's foundation, from the landing, from the black water already climbing the rocks in a way it hadn't climbed them in the two months she'd kept this light.

The gear caught cleanly on her third pass. She slammed the housing shut and threw her weight against the crank, and the mechanism groaned, resisted, then turned — slow at first, then finding its rhythm, the beam sweeping out steady into the storm exactly as the thing beneath the landing reached the base of the tower.

She heard it against the stone. Not a blow. A pressure — the sound of something enormous testing a wall it had tested before and found, on every occasion but this one, already dark.

The beam swept past the landing on the eleventh rotation and this time held on target long enough for Pia to see it whole for the first time: a shape too long for the human mind to hold as one animal, skin like wet slate catching the light and throwing it back wrong, eyes that did not reflect so much as absorb the beam entire, two black wells that had been built, it seemed to her in that frozen half-second, for exactly this — for finding the one unlit place in any given dark.

It found no unlit place tonight.

The light held its ground across the water, sweep after sweep, and the pressure against the tower's foundation did not return a second time. Somewhere near the bottom of the storm's fury, past three in the morning by her reckoning, the long low exhale sounded once more — farther out this time, farther and farther still, until the gale swallowed it whole and did not give it back.

◆ ◆ ◆

Dawn came in thin and pale, the storm spent, the sea gone flat and docile as though it had never held anything larger than a gull. Pia sat on the lamp room floor with her back against the warm brass housing, her torn fingernail throbbing in time with her pulse, and did not immediately trust the quiet enough to move.

When she finally climbed down to the landing, the overturned dinghy was gone — carried off by the gale, most likely, though she found herself glad not to know for certain. The rocks below stood scoured clean in a way two months of ordinary tides hadn't managed, scraped down to bare gray stone.

She wrote the log entry standing, before her hands could steady enough to make the handwriting look like anything but what it was.

Light held nine nights running. Gear fault found and cleared. No further sighting.

She did not write the rest — that she understood now why her father's hand had grown cramped and hurried toward the end of his final entry, or that she suspected the not-writing had less to do with running out of time than with a decision to leave the last line unfinished on purpose, a door left open rather than a door closed on something that might, on a night with a failing light, come looking for the person who'd named it.

Pia closed the logbook without finishing that thought either, and climbed back up to check the oil.
